# 1. Phenomenological Grounding: Pre-Reflective and Reflective Awareness   
### 1.1 The Core Distinction   
Phenomenology (particularly Merleau-Ponty) distinguishes two fundamental modes of self-awareness:   
|                |                                                                                                                                                                          |                                                           |
|:---------------|:-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|:----------------------------------------------------------|
|           Mode |                                                                                                                                                              Description |                                                 Character |
| Pre-Reflective | Immediate, tacit awareness inherent to any experience. The first-personal "mineness" of experience. Not a thought about experience but the feeling of experience itself. | Immediate, non-observational, tacit, passive, spontaneous |
|     Reflective |                         Higher-order act where consciousness takes itself as object. Introduces a "fracture" or "split" between the observing "I" and the observed "me." |      Mediated, thematic, objectifying, active, deliberate |

Merleau-Ponty characterizes pre-reflective awareness not as "I think" but as "I can"—a practical, bodily, action-oriented consciousness.

|:-------------------------|:-------------------------------------|:--------------------------------------|

|     Reflective Awareness |      Channel 2 (Symbolic/Dialogical) | FPN engagement, linguistic processing |
|              The "I can" |       Minimal Self, embodied subject |          Merleau-Ponty's "lived body" |

### 1.3 Implications for the Model   
The Felt Sense is pre-reflective. It is not a judgment or evaluation—it is the immediate, bodily registration of coherence or incoherence. The "spacious vs. tight" experience is the conscious readout of pre-reflective bodily self-affection mediated by the Anterior Insular Cortex.   
Reflective Prompting creates oscillation. The practice forces movement between modes:   
- Pre-reflective: The raw felt sense of an idea   
- Reflective: The articulation of that sense in dialogue   
- Pre-reflective: Checking the articulation against the body   
- Reflective: Refining based on the check   
   
The modes are sequential prerequisites:   
- You can only reflect on an experience that was already pre-reflectively present   
- Reflection illuminates and articulates what was tacit; it does not create awareness from nothing   
   
### 1.4 Felt Sense, Interoception, and Embodied Subjectivity   
### The Felt Sense as Pre-Reflective and Non-Judgmental   
The foundational layers of consciousness described in the sources correspond closely to your characterization of the Felt Sense as pre-reflective and not a judgment or evaluation.   
- Pre-Reflective Self-Awareness (PRSA): Phenomenological traditions emphasize that conscious experience inherently includes a primitive or minimal type of self-awareness which occurs prior to any deliberate, thematic analysis. This is also referred to as pre-reflective self-consciousness or first-order consciousness.   
- Tacit and Immediate: PRSA is described as a tacit, direct, and non-inferential awareness of one's experience as it is lived through. It is present in every experience without requiring introspection.   
- Non-Judgmental Quality: This fundamental awareness does not consist in forming a belief or making a judgment. Phenomenological reduction methods seek precisely to suspend these judgments, beliefs, and assumptions ("bracketing") to access experience as it is lived pre-reflectively.   
- The Felt Sense Concept: The term "felt sense—the rightness of feeling in one’s gut" is explicitly mentioned in the context of heuristic inquiry as a key component of tacit knowing. This process allows for words, phrases, or novel understandings to surface.   
   
### Bodily Registration and Self-Affection   
Your proposal that the Felt Sense is the immediate, bodily registration of coherence or incoherence aligns well with the concepts of embodiment and self-affection central to the enactive and phenomenological approaches.   
- Embodied Subjectivity: The basic self, or "minimal self," is intrinsically linked to self-affection, embodiment, and temporality. Any convincing theory of consciousness must account for this pre-reflective experience of embodied subjectivity.   
- The Lived Body (Leib): The primary engagement is tied to the subject-body (Leib), the body that the subject prereflectively lives as an incarnated subject and functions tacitly as the center and medium of experience. This bodily self-awareness is a genuine form of self-experience, not a perception of the body as an external object.   
- Self-Affection: Pre-reflective self-awareness is conceptualized in terms of self-affection (or auto-affection). This is a direct and immediate self-acquaintance that does not presuppose the mediation of any sense organ or higher-order intentional act.   
- Affective Dimension: Consciousness is fundamentally linked to bodily processes of life regulation, emotion, and affect. Lived experience is characterized by affectively valenced mental states. The term Befinden (situatedness, well- or ill-being) denotes the background feeling of the body. This background feeling captures the immediate registration of coherence or incoherence, or the general atmosphere of how things appear.   
   
### Conscious Readout and Neural Correlates (Interoception)   
Your description of the experience (e.g., "spacious vs. tight") as the conscious readout of this pre-reflective bodily self-affection is supported by the mechanism of conversion from tacit/pre-reflective experience to explicit consciousness, and the role of interoception in affective experience.   
- Accessing Tacit Experience: Pre-reflective experiences, while tacit and pre-verbal, are access conscious in principle. They can become accessible and describable through the disciplined practices of first-person methods (like the micro-phenomenological interview or Descriptive Experience Sampling). This process is described as one that intensifies, amplifies, or lights up the self-awareness already immanent in experience.   
- Interoception and Affect: The sources propose that the experience of emotion is a brain-based perception—specifically, the brain's "best guess" of the physiological condition of the body. This is tied to interoception, the sensing of signals from the interior of the body.   
- Interoceptive Inference and Selfhood:Interoceptive predictions are suggested to underlie experiences of emotion and give rise to an affectively colored sense of the embodied self. The valence quality of emotions (which includes states like "tightness" or "spaciousness") is understood in the context of the prediction's role in control and regulation.   
   
### Neural Mediation (Anterior Insular Cortex - AIC)   
The sources provide strong support for the neural function hypothesized (interoceptive processing of the embodied self and emotion) but do not specifically name the Anterior Insular Cortex (AIC) as the mediating structure.   
- Lack of Specific Citation: While the integration of interoceptive signals is identified as a potential mechanism underlying the embodied self, and is indexed in some studies using measures like the heartbeat evoked potential, the specific anatomical link to the Anterior Insular Cortex is not detailed in the provided excerpts.   
- Neurophenomenological Goal: Neurophenomenology generally aims to integrate phenomenological accounts with neurobiological accounts through reciprocal constraints. This framework would treat your hypothesis—that a specific refined experiential category ("spacious vs. tight") correlates with and helps delineate the function of a specific brain area (AIC)—as a valid avenue for research, seeking mutual validation of the experiential structure and the neuronal structures.   
   
In summary, the sources fully endorse the phenomenological structure of your idea: the Felt Sense is a non-judgmental, pre-reflective, and fundamental self-affection rooted in the lived body's basic feeling state. The scientific sources support the search for its neural basis in interoceptive integration, which is precisely the type of process Neurophenomenology attempts to connect to lived experience.
